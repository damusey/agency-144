## The Headless Migration Decision

If your D2C brand is generating over $5 million in ARR on Shopify, you have likely hit the "monolithic ceiling." 

Your store takes 5 seconds to load because of 30+ plugins. Your conversion rate is stagnating at 1.8%. Your marketing team is demanding highly custom, programmatic landing pages that the standard Shopify Liquid theme simply cannot support.

The logical next step is migrating to a **[Headless Commerce Architecture](/solutions/build)** (using Next.js as the front-end and Shopify as the back-end engine). 

But headless is not a cheap plugin you install on a Tuesday. It is a fundamental re-engineering of your digital storefront. Before you commit, you need a transparent understanding of the costs, the timeline, and the expected Return on Investment (ROI).

## Breaking Down the Migration Costs

A headless migration is not a web design project; it is a Product Engineering initiative. The cost is driven by the complexity of your custom logic, the number of third-party integrations, and the size of your product catalog.

For an established mid-market D2C brand, a professional headless migration typically ranges from **$35,000 to $80,000+**. 

Here is where that budget goes:

### 1. Front-End Engineering (Next.js)
This is the bulk of the cost. A team of [Senior React/Next.js engineers](/hire/nextjs-developers) will build your entire storefront from scratch. They must recreate every product page, collection page, cart drawer, and user account portal, ensuring perfect mobile responsiveness and sub-second load times. 
*Estimated Cost: $20,000 - $45,000*

### 2. CMS Integration (Sanity or Builder.io)
In standard Shopify, marketing teams change images and text via the Shopify theme editor. In a headless build, you need a Headless CMS (Content Management System) like Sanity or Builder.io. Engineers must architect the schemas so your marketing team can easily build custom landing pages via a drag-and-drop interface without touching code.
*Estimated Cost: $5,000 - $15,000*

### 3. Middleware & Third-Party Integrations
Standard Shopify plugins (like Yotpo for reviews, or Recharge for subscriptions) rely on Liquid templates. In headless, these plugins no longer work out-of-the-box. Engineers must manually integrate these services via their APIs. If a service lacks a robust API, a custom middleware solution must be built.
*Estimated Cost: $10,000 - $20,000*

## The Timeline: Expect 8 to 12 Weeks

A high-quality headless migration cannot be rushed. It requires strict QA testing. If the cart API fails during a Black Friday sale because the migration was rushed, the financial damage is catastrophic.

* **Weeks 1-3:** UI/UX Design & Architecture Planning.
* **Weeks 4-8:** Core Engineering (Next.js development, [Shopify Storefront API integration](/solutions/integrate)).
* **Weeks 9-10:** CMS Integration & Third-Party API wiring (Reviews, Subscriptions, Search).
* **Weeks 11-12:** QA, Load Testing, and Deployment to Vercel.

## The ROI: Why Brands Spend the Money

Why would a brand spend $60,000 to rebuild a store that is technically already "working"? Because the ROI of web performance is exponential.

### 1. The Conversion Rate Multiplier
Amazon famously proved that every 100ms of latency costs them 1% in sales. If your standard Shopify store loads in 4.5 seconds, and a Headless Next.js store loads in 0.8 seconds, the friction removed from the buying process is massive. 

For our enterprise clients, moving to headless typically yields a **15% to 30% relative increase in conversion rate**. If you are doing $500,000 a month in revenue, a 20% conversion rate bump is an extra $100,000 *per month*. The $60,000 migration pays for itself in less than 30 days.

### 2. Programmatic SEO Dominance
A Headless CMS allows you to generate thousands of hyper-specific, SEO-optimized landing pages programmatically. This drives massive amounts of free, high-intent organic traffic that standard Shopify structures struggle to capture.

### 3. Lower Customer Acquisition Costs (CAC)
When your landing pages load instantly, fewer users bounce after clicking your Meta or Google ads. This signals to the ad algorithms that your website is high-quality, which directly lowers your Cost Per Click (CPC) and dramatically improves your Return on Ad Spend (ROAS).
